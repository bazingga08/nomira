## 1. Lexicon Branding — The Firm

**Founding & basics.** Lexicon Branding was founded in **1982 by David Placek** in **Sausalito, California**, reportedly with ~$45,000 of personal savings and credit-card funding. It is a naming-only specialist firm — "focused exclusively on the development of brand names for competitive advantage." It runs lean: roughly **18 full-time staff**, ~$8M annual revenue (per the 2019 WTOP/CNN profile).
Sources: [Lexicon site](https://www.lexiconbranding.com/), [WTOP/CNN profile](https://wtop.com/business-finance/2019/09/swiffer-blackberry-dasani-meet-the-man-who-named-your-favorite-products/), [UCLA Newsroom](https://newsroom.ucla.edu/magazine/david-placek-lexicon-brand-naming)

**Track record (firm's stated figures; treat as self-reported marketing claims).** Over ~40 years: ~**3,800–4,000 brands named**; products carrying Lexicon names have generated **>$350 billion in sales**; **250+ linguists** employed over the firm's history, with a current active global network of **100+ PhD linguists across ~75–78 countries**. A proprietary morpheme database of **18,000+ morphemes** is cited in secondary coverage.
Sources: [Lenny's Newsletter](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/naming-expert-david-placek), [Lexicon linguistic-evaluations page](https://www.lexiconbranding.com/service/linguistic-cultural-evaluations/)

**David Placek background.** B.A. political science, **UCLA, 1975**; some graduate study at Georgetown; early career on a U.S. Senate Commerce Committee / political campaign, then advertising at **Foote, Cone & Belding** (San Francisco, Levi Strauss account) before founding Lexicon. The name "Lexicon" itself nods to *sound symbolism*.
Sources: [UCLA Newsroom](https://newsroom.ucla.edu/magazine/david-placek-lexicon-brand-naming), [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/david-placek-lexicon/)

**What makes them distinctive.** They position naming as a **strategic, engineered asset**, not subjective creative output. Their differentiator is an **"engineering layer"** layered onto creative: proprietary software + linguistic/cognitive science + proprietary consumer-research methods + a global linguist network + trademark expertise. Philosophy: names should be **"brilliant shortcuts to the brand promise"** and **"surprising, but surprisingly familiar"** ("the brain is lazy").
Sources: [Strategic Brand Name Development page](https://www.lexiconbranding.com/service/strategic-brand-name-development/), [UCLA](https://newsroom.ucla.edu/magazine/david-placek-lexicon-brand-naming)

### Selected name stories (verified)
- **BlackBerry (1998):** Insight from field research — mobile email *stresses* people, so the name should **calm**, not amplify urgency. "Blackberry" (keys resembled seeds/drupelets) chosen partly *because competitors lacked the courage to use it*. The **"B"** sound telegraphs reliability.
- **Swiffer (1997):** Mopping was consumers' most-hated chore. Started from sound words — *swipe, swish, swiff* — then engineered "Swiffer" for rhythm and memorability.
- **Pentium (Intel):** Committee split between safe "ProChip" and bold "Pentium." Andy Grove reportedly said the polarization signaled energy: *"that tells me there's energy for Pentium."*
- **Sonos:** A near-palindrome evoking *sound*; initially rejected internally.
- **Azure (Microsoft):** Execs called it "a dumb name"; **"Z"** draws attention.
- **Windsurf:** Generated by a "misdirection" team brainstorming *flow* concepts without knowing they were naming an AI IDE.
Sources: [Lenny's Newsletter](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/naming-expert-david-placek), [WTOP/CNN](https://wtop.com/business-finance/2019/09/swiffer-blackberry-dasani-meet-the-man-who-named-your-favorite-products/)

## 2. The Lexicon Methodology / Process

### A. The public "5-Step Brand Naming Process"
1. **Avoid groupthink.** Reject large-group brainstorming; use small, rigorous teams.
2. **Make it easy to remember.** Leverage what people already understand/visualize ("Mad Cow" beats "Bovine Spongiform Encephalopathy").
3. **Ensure trademarkability.** Beyond exact-match — paralegals, URL research, attorney review, conflict analysis.
4. **Consider linguistic & cultural implications.** *"Negative connotations, anywhere in the world, represent liabilities no brand can afford."*
5. **Use research as a decision tool — not a popularity contest.** Measure *believability* and *message delivery*, not "do you like it."
Source: [Lexicon — Brand Naming Process](https://www.lexiconbranding.com/brand-naming-process/)

### B. The 3-pillar / 3-phase creative engine (Lenny's Podcast 2025)
- **Identify (Create-strategy):** Deep conversations about how the client *behaves now* and wants to behave. Output is a behavior/experience definition, not a word list.
- **Invent:** Massive idea generation — **min ~1,000–1,500 candidate names**. Run **2–3 small teams of ~2 people**, some given **deliberately misleading briefs** to break category clichés. **Separate creation from judgment.**
- **Implement:** Linguistic + legal filters cull ~90%; ~250 "diamonds worth polishing" remain; then trademark, linguistic/cultural evaluation, consumer research, launch/messaging.

**The Diamond Framework** (DIY tool): draw a diamond labeled **Win** (top), **What we have to win** (right), **What we need to win** (bottom), **What we need to say** (left). Shifts focus from finding words to defining behavior and experience.

**Validation tactic — the "competitor test":** Don't ask "What do you think of this name?" Instead: *"Our competitor just launched with this name — what's your reaction?"*
Sources: [Lenny's Newsletter](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/naming-expert-david-placek)

### The global linguistics network
**100+ PhD linguists in ~75–78 countries**, delivering linguistic/cultural reports (assets & liabilities) in **<7 days**.
Source: [Lexicon — Linguistic & Cultural Evaluations](https://www.lexiconbranding.com/service/linguistic-cultural-evaluations/)

### Project economics (2019 reporting)
~**8 weeks**, 10–12 creative professionals + trademark research, fees ~**$50K–$150K**.
Source: [WTOP/CNN](https://wtop.com/business-finance/2019/09/swiffer-blackberry-dasani-meet-the-man-who-named-your-favorite-products/)

## 3. The Science of Naming

### Sound symbolism / phonosemantics
Vocal sounds carry meaning independent of dictionary semantics. Placek's stated letter associations:
- **V** = alive/vibrant/daring (Viagra, Corvette, Vercel)
- **B** = reliable (BlackBerry)
- **Z** = attention-drawing (Azure)
- **X** = innovation

### The Bouba/Kiki effect
Köhler (1929); popularized by **Ramachandran & Hubbard (2001)** — **~95%** pair "bouba" with rounded shapes, "kiki" with jagged. Replicated cross-culturally and in young children. Rounded/sonorant names feel *friendly, smooth, reliable*; sharp/plosive names feel *precise, modern, fast*.
Sources: [99designs](https://99designs.com/blog/design-history-movements/bouba-kiki-effect-exploring-links-sound-symbolism-design/)

### Peer-reviewed (Richard Klink, Marketing Letters 2000)
- **Front vowels** (ee, i) → smaller, lighter, milder, faster, more feminine.
- **Back vowels** (ah, o) → larger, heavier, slower, more masculine.
- **Fricatives** (f, s, v, z) → smaller, faster, lighter than **stops/plosives** (p, b, t, d, k, g).
Sources: [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1008184423824), [Yorkston & Menon, JCR](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/23547390_Phonetic_Symbolism_and_Brand_Name_Preference)

### Will Leben — Lexicon's resident linguist
Emeritus Professor of Linguistics, **Stanford** (Ph.D. MIT 1973), Lexicon hire since ~1988; built sound-symbolism/letter-structure tools and the three-step process.
Sources: [Stanford Profiles](https://profiles.stanford.edu/william-leben)

### Morphology / construction techniques
- **Real words** (suggestive): Amazon, Nike, Apple, Outback, Pivotal.
- **Compounds** ("1+1=3"): PowerBook, BlackBerry, Windsurf, OnStar.
- **Blends:** Pinterest, Microsoft, Groupon.
- **Neologisms / coined:** Sonos, Pentium, Dasani, Verizon.
- **Misspellings / truncations:** Froot Loops, Cisco, Lyft, Tumblr.
- **Foreign / classical roots:** Pent- (Greek "five") → Pentium.

### Memorability / length / rhythm
Longer, distinctive compound names can out-perform short ones via richer associations. Memorability comes from **linking to the familiar** + **visualizable imagery**, not brevity. Distinctiveness > comfort.

## 4. Practical Naming Frameworks

### Name-type taxonomy
| Type | Definition | Examples | Trademark/extensibility |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Descriptive** | States what it is | The Home Depot, General Motors | Hardest to protect; doesn't travel |
| **Suggestive / Evocative** | Real word hinting at benefit | Nike, Amazon, Outback | Sweet spot — clarity + creativity |
| **Abstract / Coined** | New word, no meaning | Sonos, Pentium, Kodak | Strongest protection; costs most to build |
| **Associative/Metaphorical** | Borrows a concept's qualities | BlackBerry, Apple | Memorable; needs a bridge |
| **Founder / Eponymous** | Person's name | Disney, Ford | Easy to trademark; limited meaning |
| **Acronym** | Letters | IBM, GE, BMW | Weak unless heavily invested |

### Criteria for a strong name
1. **Distinctive** (polarization is a positive signal) 2. **Memorable** 3. **Pronounceable/spellable** 4. **Extensible** 5. **Emotionally resonant** 6. **Trademark-available** 7. **Cross-linguistically safe** 8. **Sound-symbolically aligned**

**Alexandra Watkins' SMILE & SCRATCH test:**
- **SMILE** (great name): **S**uggestive · **M**emorable · **I**magery · **L**egs · **E**motional
- **SCRATCH** (deal-breakers): **S**pelling-challenged · **C**opycat · **R**estrictive · **A**nnoying · **T**ame · **C**urse of knowledge · **H**ard to pronounce
Source: [Hello, My Name Is Awesome](https://www.amazon.com/Hello-My-Name-Awesome-Create/dp/1523099984)

### Common pitfalls
- Expecting an **"aha" moment** (best names rarely earn instant love).
- Letting **domain availability drive the decision** (a domain is "just an area code").
- **Premature evaluation**, **groupthink**, **over-descriptive** names.

---

## 5. Screening & Validation
- **Trademark:** Multi-layer clearance by class/jurisdiction. Coined/abstract clear most easily.
- **Domain:** Check, but don't let it veto a superior name; budget to acquire or use alt-TLDs (.ai).
- **Linguistic disaster checks:** Native-speaker review across all operating markets.
- **Consumer testing:** Frame for message delivery/believability, not preference — use the *competitor test*.
