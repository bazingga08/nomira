## 2. The Process

Lexicon runs naming as **three structurally separated phases** — IDENTIFY → INVENT → IMPLEMENT — that deliberately keep strategy, creation, and judgment apart. *Confirmed (Placek verbatim).* The public-facing "5-Step Process" on the site is a complementary framing. Use both.
- Source: [Focus Lab — Brand Naming in 2026](https://www.focuslab.agency/blog/brand-naming-in-2026-ai-trademarks-emerging-trends), [Lenny's Newsletter](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/naming-expert-david-placek)

### A. The 3-phase engine

**Phase 1 — IDENTIFY (strategy).** 2–3 stakeholder meetings focused on **future desired behavior/experience** (not the current mission). Output is a loose **"creative framework"** — Placek calls it a *"metaphor for a window,"* a direction, **not tight objectives** (no letter-count rules). Plus a competitive study. Guiding mantra: *"Imagine the end and work backwards."*

**Phase 2 — INVENT (creation).** A ~3-week creative cycle. Linguists mine **morphemes / prefixes / suffixes / infixes**. **Suspend judgment** and generate abundantly — target **~1,000–1,500 candidates** (more for big projects; some accounts say 2,000–3,000) before *any* filtering, then shortlist to **50–100**. Rigorous trademark analysis runs concurrently. Optional consumer research (~50% of projects).

**Phase 3 — IMPLEMENT (judgment).** The global linguist network performance-profiles finalists across languages (assets/liabilities). Names are presented with an internal rationale so stakeholders can advocate. Trademark clearance, cross-linguistic screening, and consumer testing finalize the choice.

### B. The public "5-Step Brand Naming Process" (verbatim headings)
1. **Avoid "groupthink."** *"Traditional brainstorming isn't effective. You must avoid the need for comfort or logic."* Small teams significantly outperform large groups.
2. **Make it easy to remember.** *"There is science behind memory. All of us remember what we understand and what we can visualize."* ("Mad Cow" beats "Bovine Spongiform Encephalopathy.")
3. **Ensure your name is trademarkable.** Go beyond a standard knockout search — involve paralegals, a URL-search team, and a trademark attorney.
4. **Consider linguistic implications, even in countries where you don't operate.** *"In the digital world, your brand name is instantly global. Negative connotations, anywhere in the world, represent liabilities that no brand can afford."*
5. **Use research as a decision-making tool.** Research *"adds an objective lens to decision-making"*; a winning name *"does not need to be comfortable or popular."*
- Source: [Brand Naming Process](https://www.lexiconbranding.com/brand-naming-process/) *Confirmed.*

### C. The Diamond Framework (founder briefing tool)
Draw a diamond and answer four questions to fix the **name's job** vs design's and messaging's jobs:
1. What does winning look like? (How do we define winning?)
2. What do we **have** to win already?
3. What do we **need** to win?
4. What do we need to **say**?
Requires several days of iterative discussion, not one session.
- Source: [podpulse — Lenny's Podcast notes](https://podpulse.ai/podcast-notes-and-takeaways/lennys-podcast-product-growth-career-naming-expert-shares-the-process-behind-creating-billion-dollar-brand-names-like-azure-vercel-windsurf-sonos-blackberry-and-impossible-burger-david-placek-lexicon-branding)

### D. Project economics & 2026 realities
- ~8–10 weeks; 10–12 people; fee $50K–$150K (per Lexicon / CNN). [CNN/KOAM](https://www.koamnewsnow.com/meet-the-man-who-named-your-favorite-products/)
- **Trademark saturation is the binding constraint:** US Nice Class 9 had ~23,000 marks when Placek started, now **well over 2 million** (vs ~500,000 dictionary words). Best-case ~9 months to registration; file ~3 years before launch. [Focus Lab 2026](https://www.focuslab.agency/blog/brand-naming-in-2026-ai-trademarks-emerging-trends)
- **AI is used for JUDGMENT, not generation:** "seven small language models trained on Lexicon's historical research" profile names for processing fluency/memorability to inject objectivity. Final selection stays human. [Focus Lab 2026](https://www.focuslab.agency/blog/brand-naming-in-2026-ai-trademarks-emerging-trends)
- **AI products should avoid anthropomorphic/human names** (prevents overpromising) and overly technical terms (Codeium → Windsurf). [Antoine Buteau notes](https://www.antoinebuteau.com/lessons-from-david-placek/)

## 3. The Master Judging Heuristics

These are the rules for *choosing* among generated candidates. Encode all five.

**H1 — "Surprising, but surprisingly familiar."** *"The brain is lazy. It likes the familiar."* Score every finalist on TWO axes — (a) distinctiveness/surprise AND (b) instant decodability/processing-fluency — and require BOTH. Build familiarity from high-frequency morphemes recombined in a novel way.
- Source: [UCLA Newsroom](https://newsroom.ucla.edu/magazine/david-placek-lexicon-brand-naming) *Confirmed.*

**H2 — Polarization & discomfort are BUY signals.** *"If your team is comfortable with the name, chances are you don't have the name yet."* Split opinion = "energy." Pentium split the room (Andy Grove: *"there's energy for Pentium here"*); Azure was called *"a dumb idea"* by a senior Microsoft exec. Never eliminate a candidate solely because people dislike it on first hearing. There is *no* instant "aha" — *"you won't know it when you see it."*
- Sources: [Lenny X post](https://x.com/lennysan/status/1939793959024492805), [Azure X post](https://x.com/lennysan/status/1939729305132736942), [FounderBrief summary](https://founderbrief.com/summary/how-iconic-brand-names-are-created) *Confirmed.*

**H3 — Communicate an original idea; NEVER describe.** *"The best names don't describe reality; they change it."* Prefer invented/connotative names over descriptive/suggestive ones (Google beats Infoseek). Descriptive names commoditize and age badly (Diehard, "cloud X"). Counter-myth: *"Invented names like Pentium actually take less money to build into brands than existing words."*
- Sources: [Lenny — How to find the perfect name](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/how-to-find-the-perfect-name), [Lexicon — Myths](https://www.lexiconbranding.com/myths-that-stop-remarkable-names/), [Focus Lab](https://www.focuslab.agency/blog/brand-naming) *Confirmed.*

**H4 — The name is a compounding strategic ASSET.** It outlives design, messaging, and product, delivering **asymmetric / cumulative advantage** ("not like the other guys"). *"The right name is a competitive advantage that no one can take away from you."* Lead the process; don't manage it. Rename only with a genuine catalyst (changed business model) and a clear consumer "what's in it for me."
- Source: [podpulse](https://podpulse.ai/podcast-notes-and-takeaways/lennys-podcast-product-growth-career-naming-expert-shares-the-process-behind-creating-billion-dollar-brand-names-like-azure-vercel-windsurf-sonos-blackberry-and-impossible-burger-david-placek-lexicon-branding) *Confirmed.*

**H5 — "Relevant, but unexpected" construction.** Start from a literal, on-strategy concept ("fresh breeze") and **distort it into a distinctive coined form** ("Febreze"). Don't stop at the obvious literal phrase. Name to the **consumer-emotion or category-belief problem**, not the mechanism (Febreze solves disbelief that "fresheners just mask odors"; Swiffer "puts joy into" a hated chore).
- Source: [Febreze case study](https://www.lexiconbranding.com/case-studies/product-names-pg-febreze/) *Confirmed.*

## 4. The Science: Sound Symbolism & Phonosemantics

**Core thesis (Lexicon's signature science).** Every letter/sound carries a "psychological vibration" — meaning independent of dictionary definition. The **first letter holds the most symbolic power** (primacy). Effects hold cross-linguistically (US/Europe/Asia). *Confirmed* (Lexicon framing + independent NYT corroboration).
- Sources: [podpulse](https://podpulse.ai/podcast-notes-and-takeaways/lennys-podcast-product-growth-career-naming-expert-shares-the-process-behind-creating-billion-dollar-brand-names-like-azure-vercel-windsurf-sonos-blackberry-and-impossible-burger-david-placek-lexicon-branding), [NYT Magazine — Weird Science of Naming](https://www.nytimes.com/2015/01/18/magazine/the-weird-science-of-naming-new-products.html), [Branding Mag — Placek on sound symbolism](https://www.brandingmag.com/david-placek/the-science-of-sound-symbolism-and-the-importance-of-your-brand-name/)

**Academic backbone (peer-reviewed):**
- **Klink (2000), *Creating Brand Names With Meaning*, Marketing Letters 11(1):5–20.** Phonemes convey product attributes (size, speed, strength, weight) even for nonsense names, with or without supporting marketing. [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1008184423824) · [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/225886900_Creating_Brand_Names_With_Meaning_The_Use_of_Sound_Symbolism) *Confirmed.*
  - Front-vowel names judged smaller/lighter/milder/thinner/softer/faster/colder/less-bitter/more-feminine/prettier than back-vowel names.
  - Initial **stops** read smaller/faster/lighter/more-feminine than **fricatives**; **voiceless stops** smaller/faster/lighter/sharper than **voiced stops**.
  - **Vowels are a stronger size signal than consonants**: higher-frequency (front) vowels read "smaller" ~81% of the time vs 55–76% for consonants.
- **Yorkston & Menon (2004), *A Sound Idea*, JCR 31(1):43–51.** Identical ice-cream press release named **"Frosh"** (back vowel /a/) vs **"Frish"** (front vowel /i/); "Frosh" rated smoother/creamier/richer. Effect is **automatic, non-conscious, and STRONGER under cognitive load/distraction** (participants couldn't discount it when impaired: Frosh M=62.4 vs Frish M=50.0). [Stanford PDF](https://web.stanford.edu/class/linguist62n/yorkston.pdf) · [JCR](https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article-abstract/31/1/43/1812051) *Confirmed (verbatim from PDF).*
- **Lowrey & Shrum (2007), JCR 34(3):406–414.** **Fit beats intensity:** front-vowel names win ~2:1 for small/light/sharp products (convertible, knife); back-vowel names win ~2:1 for large/heavy products (SUV, hammer). An incongruent name is actively *dispreferred*. [JCR](https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article-abstract/34/3/406/1798924) *Confirmed.*
- **Klink & Athaide (2012), Marketing Letters.** Front vowels build feminine/sophisticated/sincere personality; back vowels build masculine/rugged. Gender-congruent sound raises preference. [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/227451737_Creating_brand_personality_with_brand_names) *Confirmed.*
- **Pathak/Calvert (2020), Psychology & Marketing.** Adding a **late-acquired phoneme** or **affricate** (/tʃ/ ch, /dʒ/ j) can flip perception basic→luxury. [NTU release](https://www.ntu.edu.sg/business/news-events/news/story-detail/luxury-brands-and-linguistics-what-does-luxury-sound-like) *Confirmed (medium — sourced via release/abstracts, exact magnitudes unverified).*

**Bouba/kiki effect.** Rounded vowels (/u/,/o/) + voiced stops + sonorants → round shapes; high-front unrounded vowels (/i/) + voiceless stops (/k/,/t/,/p/) → spiky shapes. Köhler (1929, takete/maluma); Ramachandran & Hubbard (2001, bouba/kiki). Consonants drive the shape mapping more than vowels.
- Lab agreement ~95–98% (Ramachandran & Hubbard). [Wikipedia — Bouba/kiki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouba/kiki_effect) *Confirmed.*
- **Cross-cultural test (Ćwiek et al., 2022):** 917 participants, 25 languages, 10 scripts; **71.1%** matched bouba=round / kiki=spiky; 17/25 languages validated it. **Caveat:** Romanian, Mandarin, Turkish fell at/below chance (native lexicon can override the mapping). Effect largely orthography-independent. [Phil. Trans. R. Soc. B](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rstb/article/377/1841/20200390/108730/The-bouba-kiki-effect-is-robust-across-cultures) *Confirmed.*

**Will Leben** — emeritus Stanford linguist, Lexicon's resident sound-symbolism scientist; co-built the morpheme/phoneme tools. (His own Psychology Today articles were 403-blocked this run; cited via secondary coverage.) [NYT Magazine](https://www.nytimes.com/2015/01/18/magazine/the-weird-science-of-naming-new-products.html)

**Replication discipline.** Treat these as **ranked priors, not laws.** Size/shape (vowel front-back, bouba/kiki) are large and innate-leaning. Gender, luxury, and taste effects are smaller and context-dependent. Always screen candidates against the target market's lexicon and validate with real-audience testing before launch.

## 5. Phoneme / Sound → Connotation Table (OPERATIONAL)

Transcribe a candidate to IPA, tag each sound, and weight the **stressed-syllable vowel** and the **initial onset consonant** most heavily.

### Vowels (strongest, most reliable lever — ~81% size accuracy)
| Sound | IPA | Connotation | Use for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Front/high vowels | /i/ (ee), /ɪ/ (ih), /e/ | small, light, fast, thin, mild, cold, sharp, pretty, crisp/fresh, feminine | tech, gadgets, light/diet foods, agile/fast products |
| Back/low vowels | /a/ (ah), /o/ (oh), /u/ (oo) | large, heavy, slow, thick, rich, creamy, smooth, dark, rugged, masculine | SUVs, durable goods, indulgent foods, premium/heavy |

### Consonants — manner
| Class | Sounds | Connotation | Use for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Voiceless stops | p, t, k | small, fast, light, sharp, crisp, energetic, techy | precision, speed, edge |
| Voiced stops | b, d, g | large, heavy, slow, solid, round, friendly, potent | trust, substance, dependability (B = "most reliable") |
| Fricatives | f, s, v, z | small/fast/light + smooth, flowing, continuous, speed | flow, energy, motion |
| Sonorants | l, m, n, r, w, h | soft, smooth, gentle, soothing, comforting, melodic | comfort, luxury, calm, round feel |
| Affricates | tʃ (ch), dʒ (j) | luxury, novelty, distinctiveness (late-acquired) | premium/luxury positioning |

### Signature single-letter cues (Lexicon / Placek)
| Letter | Connotation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| V | "most alive and vibrant" sound; more V's = more energetic | Vercel, Corvette |
| B | "one of the most reliable" sounds | BlackBerry |
| Z | noisy/energetic, attention-drawing | Azure |
| X | fast, crisp, innovative | (innovation cue) |
- Source: [podpulse](https://podpulse.ai/podcast-notes-and-takeaways/lennys-podcast-product-growth-career-naming-expert-shares-the-process-behind-creating-billion-dollar-brand-names-like-azure-vercel-windsurf-sonos-blackberry-and-impossible-burger-david-placek-lexicon-branding), [Branding Mag](https://www.brandingmag.com/david-placek/the-science-of-sound-symbolism-and-the-importance-of-your-brand-name/)

### Bouba/kiki shape cue
- **Round / soft / friendly / organic:** rounded vowels (/o/,/u/) + voiced stops + sonorants (bouba/maluma).
- **Sharp / angular / edgy / high-tech:** /i/,/ɪ/ + voiceless stops (kiki/takete).

### Application rules
- **Pick sound to match the ONE primary benefit, then validate** (don't intuit — Frish/Frosh shows vowel choice shifts perceived quality automatically).
- **Congruence over intensity:** reinforce a single desired attribute; avoid mixing contradictory cues (front-vowel "small" + voiced-stop "heavy" dilutes the signal).
- **Fit-to-category (Lowrey & Shrum):** don't maximize one attribute blindly; match the connoted attribute to what is *desirable* for the product.

## 6. Phonesthemes & Morpheme Toolkit

**Phonesthemes** (sub-morphemic clusters with consistent meaning; term coined by J.R. Firth, 1930). Use desirable clusters; screen out pejorative ones. [Wikipedia — Phonestheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonestheme) *Confirmed.*
| Cluster | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| gl- | light / vision / shine | glow, glitter, glisten, gleam, glint |
| fl- | quick / light movement | fly, flap, flutter, flicker, flash, flow |
| str- | lines / tension / strength | string, strap, streak, stripe, stretch, stream |
| sn- | nose / mouth | sniff, snuff, snore, snout, sneeze, snack |
| sl- | slippery (and **pejorative — avoid**) | slime, slip, slide, sludge, sleazy |
| -ash | forceful sudden impact | crash, smash, dash, splash, slash |
| -ump | roundness / heaviness | lump, bump, hump, plump, clump |
| wr- | twisting / distortion | wring, wrap, wrench, wriggle, wrinkle |

**Morpheme construction (the Lexicon engine).** Combine meaning-bearing roots + connotation-bearing affixes from a morpheme library. Aim for compounds that yield **"1+1=3"** (a third emergent meaning).
- Pentium = **Pent** (5th gen) + **-ium** (scientific element ending, cf. sodium/uranium)
- Vercel = **ver** (Latin *truth/green*) + **cel** (accelerate); built from 55 prefixes × 102 suffixes, ~60 concepts, 3 cycles over 2 weeks
- Dasani = crisp **da** + **san** (Latin *health*) + light **i**
- Windsurf = wind + surf → a third, tangible meaning for an intangible AI IDE
- Source: [Lenny — How to find the perfect name](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/how-to-find-the-perfect-name) *(morpheme etymologies are paraphrase/medium-confidence)*

## 7. Name-Type Taxonomy

### A. Creative taxonomy (Igor + Catchword + Lexicon)
| Type | Definition | Examples | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Functional / Descriptive** | Says what it does | InfoSeek, General Motors | "Lowest common denominator"; weak, ages badly |
| **Experiential** | Direct connection to a human experience, literal with imagination | Explorer, Navigator, Safari | Concrete, relatable |
| **Evocative** | Evokes the *positioning*; removed from direct experience but relevant | Virgin, Apple, Yahoo, Uber | Igor's recommended type — "powerful differentiators," best trademark prospects, hardest to approve |
| **Invented (neologism / empirical-classical)** | Coined / made-up, or built from Greek/Latin | Google, Oreo, Sonos, Pentium, Verizon | Strongest protection; needs phonetic engineering to carry meaning |
- Source: [Igor Naming Guide 2022A (PDF)](https://www.igorinternational.com/process/i/Igor%20Naming%20Guide%202022A.pdf), [Catchword via How Brands Are Built](https://howbrandsarebuilt.com/types-of-brand-names/)

### B. Legal distinctiveness spectrum (trademark backbone)
**Fanciful > Arbitrary > Suggestive > Descriptive > Generic** (strongest → weakest protection).
| Class | Definition | Examples | Protectability |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fanciful / Coined | Invented, no prior meaning | Kodak, Exxon, Xerox, Verizon | Strongest; inherently distinctive |
| Arbitrary | Real word, unrelated context | Apple (computers), Amazon, Camel | Inherently distinctive |
| Suggestive | Needs imagination to connect | Netflix, Coppertone, Blu-ray, Jaguar | Inherently distinctive (most popular style) |
| Descriptive | Directly describes | Holiday Inn, American Airlines, Sharp | Protectable only with secondary meaning |
| Generic | Common name for the goods | "Computer," "Email" | Never protectable |
- Source: [Wikipedia — Trademark distinctiveness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trademark_distinctiveness), [Wikipedia — Trademark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trademark) *Confirmed.*

**Bias:** generate away from Descriptive/Generic; toward Evocative/Arbitrary/Suggestive for the best memorability + trademarkability balance; allow Fanciful/Coined **only if** sound-symbolism-validated AND it passes the SCRATCH spelling/pronunciation gates.

## 8. Generation Playbook

### A. Word-formation techniques (pick a technique per batch)
| Technique | How | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Arbitrary real word | Real word in unrelated category | Apple, Amazon, Orange |
| Compound | Two whole words (both stems intact) | Facebook, PayPal, PowerBook |
| Blend / portmanteau | Clip ≥1 word, join at a shared junction | Netflix (net+flicks), Groupon, Brexit |
| Classical root coinage | Latin/Greek roots | Volvo ("I roll"), Audi ("listen"), Acura |
| Respelling / misspelling | Distort a real word | Google (<googol), Flickr, Lyft |
| Pure neologism | Fully invented | Kodak, Xerox, Verizon |
- Source: [Wikipedia — Word formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_formation), [Wikipedia — Blend word](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blend_word) *Confirmed.*
- Note: a **compound** preserves both full stems; a **blend** clips at least one.

### B. The three-teams method (core create-vs-judge mechanic)
Spin up multiple small teams; **only one knows the true brief.** Others believe they're naming a competitor (working "for Apple" when the client is Microsoft) or an unrelated category (e.g., a bicycle). Winners disproportionately come from the **blind teams** because they're unconstrained. (Windsurf came from a team merely listing "flow" concepts.)
- Encode: generate from **oblique/adjacent prompts**, then judge against the true brief in a **separate pass**.
- Source: [podpulse](https://podpulse.ai/podcast-notes-and-takeaways/lennys-podcast-product-growth-career-naming-expert-shares-the-process-behind-creating-billion-dollar-brand-names-like-azure-vercel-windsurf-sonos-blackberry-and-impossible-burger-david-placek-lexicon-branding) *Confirmed.*

### C. Generation principles
- **Suspend judgment; name abundantly.** Target ~1,000–1,500 raw candidates before ANY filtering, then shortlist 50–100. Quantity drives quality. (Anthony Shore: *"to name well, you must name abundantly."*) [How Brands Are Built — Shore](https://howbrandsarebuilt.com/podcast/anthony-shore/)
- **Explore concepts, not words** (Shore): seed from underlying concepts/positioning — *"concepts can include other concepts"* and are more generative. Ladder via word-association and metaphor.
- **Thesaurus-chain technique** (Watkins): write 12 brand adjectives → pick one → expand all synonyms → pick one; run association ladders (cold→old→gold). [Nat Eliason notes on Watkins](https://www.nateliason.com/notes/hello-name-awesome)
- **Alternate immersion (focused generation) with incubation (pause)** between rounds (Shore).
- Use the **morpheme engine** (§6) and **sound toolkit** (§5) to build candidates against the target attribute profile.

## 9. Scoring Rubric (criteria + weights)

Apply after SCRATCH hard-gates (§10). Score each surviving candidate; suggested weights total 100. Tune per project.

| # | Criterion | What it measures | Weight | Source basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Distinctiveness / surprise** | Stands apart from category; polarizing is good (H2) | 18 | Lexicon H1/H2 |
| 2 | **Processing fluency / familiarity** | Decodable on first hearing; "surprisingly familiar" (H1) | 15 | Lexicon H1 |
| 3 | **Memorability / imagery** | Visualizable; links to the familiar (5-Step #2; SMILE "I") | 14 | Lexicon + Watkins |
| 4 | **Sound-symbolism fit** | Phonemes reinforce the ONE target attribute (§5) | 12 | Klink, Yorkston, Lowrey |
| 5 | **Strategic / emotional resonance** | Names the consumer belief/emotion problem (H5); SMILE "E" | 12 | Lexicon + Watkins |
| 6 | **Trademark strength** | Position on Fanciful>Arbitrary>Suggestive spectrum | 12 | TM spectrum |
| 7 | **Cross-linguistic safety** | No negative meaning in any target market | 8 | Lexicon 5-Step #4 |
| 8 | **Extensibility / legs** | Extends to sub-brands, taglines, brand world (SMILE "L") | 6 | Watkins / A Hundred Monkeys |
| 9 | **Pronounceability / spellability** | Correct on first attempt (phone test) | 3 | SCRATCH "H/S" |

**SMILE positive overlay (Watkins, 0–2 each):** **S**uggestive · **M**emorable · **I**magery (note: *Imagery*, not "imaginative") · **L**egs · **E**motional. Require minimum thresholds on Suggestive + Emotional + Imagery (the stickiness drivers).
- Source: [Eat My Words — SMILE/SCRATCH](https://eatmywords.com/smile-scratch-test/) *Confirmed (verbatim).*

**Story / "ask-a-question" test (A Hundred Monkeys):** each finalist must (a) support a one-sentence story of *why this name*, (b) make a listener want to ask a follow-up question, and (c) extend into a brand world. Reject names with no narrative.
- Source: [A Hundred Monkeys](https://www.ahundredmonkeys.com/)

## 10. Screening Checklist

### Gate 0 — SCRATCH hard-rejects (Watkins) — apply BEFORE scoring
Auto-reject any candidate that is:
- **S**pelling-challenged (looks like a typo)
- **C**opycat (too close to a competitor)
- **R**estrictive (boxes in future growth)
- **A**nnoying (forced/contrived)
- **T**ame (flat, descriptive, boring)
- **C**urse of knowledge (only insiders get it)
- **H**ard to pronounce
- Source: [Eat My Words — SMILE/SCRATCH](https://eatmywords.com/smile-scratch-test/) *Confirmed.*

### Gate 1 — Trademark knockout
- Search the national trademark database for marks similar in **SOUND, APPEARANCE, or MEANING** on **related goods/services**; reject likelihood-of-confusion hits. [USPTO Trademark Search](https://www.uspto.gov/trademarks/search) *Confirmed.*
- Go **beyond a knockout search** (Lexicon 5-Step #3): paralegals + URL-search team + trademark attorney.

### Gate 2 — Class scoping
- Identify which of the **45 Nice classes** (1–34 goods, 35–45 services) apply; clearance is **per-class**. Screen relevant + adjacent classes. [WIPO Nice](https://www.wipo.int/classifications/nice/en/) *Confirmed.*

### Gate 3 — Jurisdiction
- Trademark rights are **territorial**. Plan per country/region or via the **Madrid System** (one filing, up to 131 countries, requires a home base mark). [WIPO Madrid](https://www.wipo.int/madrid/en/) *Confirmed.*

### Gate 4 — Linguistic / cultural disaster check
- Native-speaker / dictionary review in EVERY target-market language (Lexicon screens even non-operating markets — a name is "instantly global"). Cautionary cases: **Mitsubishi Pajero**, **Honda Fitta**, **Ford Pinto**. (Note: Chevy Nova "no va" and Coca-Cola "wax tadpole" are partly apocryphal — verify, don't repeat.) [Wikipedia — Sound symbolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_symbolism)

### Gate 5 — Domain & handles
- Check exact-match .com and social handles, BUT **do NOT let .com availability veto a strong name.** Lexicon's own research found most consumers don't notice or care about URL extension (only ~28% pay attention to the URL; ~42% don't remember the extension). The ".com requirement" is outdated "area code" thinking — secure the name first, buy/prefix the domain later (get-, try-, -app, -hq, alt-TLDs).
- Sources: [PRWeb — Lexicon URL research](https://www.prweb.com/releases/new-research-from-lexicon-branding-finds-most-consumers-dont-notice--or-care-about--a-companys-url-extension-302737912.html), [Lexicon — Debunking the URL myth](https://www.lexiconbranding.com/insights/) *Confirmed.*

### Gate 6 — Consumer validation (decision tool, not popularity)
- Frame for **message delivery & believability**, not "do you like it." Use the **competitor test:** *"Our competitor just launched with this name — what's your reaction?"* A winning name "does not need to be comfortable or popular." [Brand Naming Process](https://www.lexiconbranding.com/brand-naming-process/)
- Say-it-out-loud / phone test: if it can't be spelled after one hearing or pronounced after one reading, downgrade it.

### Full process order
Brief (Diamond) → competitive taxonomy mapping → abundant generation (three-teams, suspended judgment) → SCRATCH gates → score on rubric (§9) → trademark knockout + class/jurisdiction → linguistic + domain/handle → competitor test + consumer reaction → comprehensive legal clearance on 1–3 finalists.

## 11. Competing Frameworks & Where Lexicon Diverges

| Camp | Stance on invented/coined names | Method signature |
|---|---|---|
| **Lexicon / Anthony Shore / Catchword** | Embrace **engineered coinages** via sound symbolism & morpheme formulas | Phonetic engineering; morpheme library; Shore uses a 35B-word corpus + RNN |
| **Watkins (SMILE/SCRATCH) / Igor / A Hundred Monkeys** | **Anti-invented**; prefer real words recontextualized | "Spelling-challenged" is a SCRATCH dealbreaker; Igor "militantly" pro-real-word, anti-focus-group, anti-URL-driven |

**Cross-framework agreement (universal screens):** distinctive, memorable, meaningful, positive, protectable, scalable, pronounceable, available; linguistic + cultural screening; trademark clearance is the most critical checkpoint; descriptive/generic names are weak; the name must serve strategy/positioning.
- Sources: [How Brands Are Built — Shore](https://howbrandsarebuilt.com/podcast/anthony-shore/), [Operative Words](https://www.operativewords.com/about), [Labbrand — 7 criteria](https://www.labbrand.com/insights/7-criteria-a-top-naming-agency-uses-to-judge-a-strong-brand-name.html)

**Conflict-resolution rule for this skill:** treat invented/coined names as **ALLOWED but gated** — they must pass SCRATCH (spelling + pronounceability) AND a sound-symbolism validation tying phonetics to the desired connotation; otherwise default to evocative real-word / arbitrary names.

- **Vercel** — "ver" (truth) + "cel" (accelerate); chosen for a fast, "alive, daring" sound.

- **Swiffer** — research found "no one liked mopping"; reframed to fun; built from "swipe/swish/swiff"; won >80% vs "Promop/EffiClean." West: "Lexicon... invented the Swiffer, because without its name it would not be the same product."
- **BlackBerry** — mobile email *stresses* people, so the name should calm; keys resembled seeds/drupelets; "B" = reliability.
- **Dasani** — "da" + "san" (health) + light "i"; coined to break bottled-water place-name conventions.
- **Navan** — palindrome enabling expansion beyond travel.
