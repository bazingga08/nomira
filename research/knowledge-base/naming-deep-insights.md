## 1. The Naming Firm as an Org Chart (how to staff/replicate a firm)

### 1.1 Two viable org models

**A. Lean boutique / solo-expert (clone this FIRST)**
- One **Lead Namer** owns the entire creative pipeline (brief → generation → shortlist → rationale).
- Specialists are **rented on demand, not standing headcount**: a linguistic/cross-language screener and a trademark-knockout searcher, invoked per-stage.
- Real examples: Operative Words = Anthony Shore alone (ex-Global Director of Naming & Writing at Landor, 13 yrs), personally does creative work, collaborates with trademark attorneys + linguists as needed; Eat My Words = Alexandra Watkins-led; A Hundred Monkeys = small boutique.
- **Agent-org mapping:** one Lead-Namer agent + on-demand `linguistic-screener` and `trademark-knockout` agents. Add Naming Strategist, rationale-writer, and PM agents only when scaling.

**B. Scaled / enterprise (only when bundling broader brand strategy)**
- Org chart: **Naming Strategist → Lead Namer / Creative Director → Namers/Writers → Linguist + Trademark/Legal → Project Manager.**
- Lexicon itself runs an **in-house trademark team (3 paralegals + 1 attorney)** clearing in parallel, plus **107 contracted linguists globally** and **7 proprietary small language models** trained on prior research (used to score/profile candidates for objectivity, NOT to replace creative/strategy/legal judgment).
- Big-three (Interbrand, Landor, Siegel+Gale) treat naming as a **sub-service of brand strategy / verbal identity**, staffed by multi-disciplinary teams (strategists, verbal-identity/naming specialists, designers, researchers). "Global Director of Naming & Writing" is a real title; "Brand Naming Specialist" is a recognized discrete job. Verbal-identity scope spans naming, nomenclature, brandlines, messaging, brand voice.

### 1.2 Pricing tiers (defines where an AI firm undercuts)

### 1.3 Productize as a single fixed-price/scope/timeline SKU
Model on Igor's $45k / 6–8 week all-inclusive package. Bundle: competitive audit + positioning, brand brief, name development (hundreds of candidates), evaluation/shortlist, preliminary US trademark screening, final presentation with rationale, domain-acquisition consultation. **Scope = the agent pipeline.** List tagline and visual identity as priced add-ons.

### 1.4 The deliverable is a PRESENTATION, not a list
Across all firms the final deliverable bundles: (a) shortlist of 5–25 names, (b) per-name **strategic rationale tied back to the brief**, (c) names shown **in context** (mockups/scenarios), not naked on a list. Implies a dedicated rationale-writer agent + a mockup/visualization step as the final production stage.

## 2. The Canonical Industry Pipeline (5–8 stages, near-identical across all firms)

Cross-validated across Igor, How Brands Are Built, Catchword, Eleken:

1. **Discovery / Brief + Competitive Audit** (mission/values, not just operations; audit rivals' names first — differentiation is relative)
2. **Naming Strategy** — criteria + "territories" (strategic/emotional angles)
3. **Name Generation** — high-volume creative, NO self-editing
4. **Evaluation / Shortlisting** — score against pre-set criteria
5. **Trademark pre-screening / knockout** — BEFORE client presentation ("don't fall in love with a name you can't own")
6. **Linguistic & cultural check** — native speakers
7. **Client Presentation** — rationale + in-context mockups
8. **Selection & validation / final clearance**

**Funnel economics:** generate hundreds–thousands → ruthlessly filter to 5–25. Lexicon 2,000–3,000+; Catchword ~2,000; Operative Words/Landor "thousands"; expect ~90% to die in screening (hence volume is *required*, not optional).

### 2.1 Generate around "territories," never random brainstorming
Igor taxonomy of name types (a required upstream generation input): **Founder/heritage; Descriptive/Functional; Invented (Greek/Latin constructions vs. poetic "empty vessel" like Kodak/Xerox); Experiential (Explorer, Excursion); Evocative/Metaphorical (Amazon, Yahoo, Virgin — Igor's favorite).** Anthony Shore calls this "structured creativity": systematic exploration of strategic/emotional territories so the space is covered comprehensively.

### 3.1 Where firms CONVERGE (the consensus playbook)
Brief/positioning first → massive-volume generation with judgment deferred → multi-stage trademark + linguistic/cultural screening → validation (but don't over-trust it). Shared finalist criteria: **distinctive/differentiated, meaningful/story-bearing, memorable, future-proof/extendable, protectable.**

### 3.2 Where firms DIFFER
- **Descriptive names:** Igor, Lexicon, Operative Words actively discourage (literal/aging/unownable). Siegel+Gale tolerates ("Do use descriptive terms").
- **Team model:** big-team (Lexicon, Catchword, Landor) vs. solo-expert curation (Operative Words).
- **Selection:** all warn against focus groups / crowd-voting to *select*; use one-on-one interviews + expert criteria-based judgment; research informs, never decides.

### 3.3 Proprietary evaluation frameworks (each firm's QA gate + marketing IP)
- **Eat My Words SMILE & SCRATCH** — SMILE = **S**uggestive, **M**emorable, **I**maginative, **L**egs, **E**motional. SCRATCH deal-breakers = **S**pelling-challenged, **C**opycat, **R**estrictive, **A**nnoying, **T**asteless/**T**ame, **C**onfusing/**C**urse-of-knowledge, **H**ard-to-pronounce. (Directly encodable: scoring dims + auto-reject disqualifiers.)
- **Igor** — "Context is everything": test names as they'd appear in use, not as a list.
- **Lexicon** — sound symbolism + diamond framework.

> **Takeaway:** an AI naming firm needs its OWN named, codifiable rubric that is simultaneously the filter and the brand differentiator.

## 4. The Academic Curriculum Behind Naming Expertise

No university degree *is* "naming." Expertise is assembled from four linguistics subfields + applied marketing/sound-symbolism literature. The reusable value of a naming skill is that it **encodes the pipeline practitioners otherwise assemble ad hoc.**

### 4.1 Onomastics (the discipline of proper names)
- Scholars = "onomasticians." Branches: **toponymy** (places), **anthroponymy** (people), **literary onomastics**, **socio-onomastics**, and **ergonymy/chrematonyms = commercial/brand names** (brand naming lives here).
- University syllabi (Adam Mickiewicz, Uppsala, Glasgow, Nottingham, Newcastle) teach: proper-names-vs-appellatives, categories of onyms, **etymology vs. motivation** (motivation = how transparently the name connects to its referent), name use & identity-building, and explicitly "commercial names." Assessment ≈ 3,000-word project.
- **Encodable:** classify each candidate as a chrematonym; score on **motivation** (transparent↔opaque connection) and identity-signalling, not just etymology. Specialized onomastics courses are rare and usually place-name-focused — exactly why an encoded brand-naming pipeline captures scattered expertise.

### 4.2 Phonetics & phonology (judging how a name sounds)
- Core textbooks: Ladefoged & Johnson *A Course in Phonetics*; Clark/Yallop/Fletcher; Hayes *Introductory Phonology*; Collins & Mees *Practical Phonetics and Phonology*.
- Concepts: phoneme, IPA, articulatory description (place/manner/voicing; vowel height/backness/rounding), syllable structure (onset/nucleus/coda/rime), Sonority Sequencing Principle, stress, prosody, connected speech.
- **Phonotactics / wordlikeness (gradient):** English speakers rank *brick* (real) > *blick* (possible) > *bnick* (impossible). Constraints are **language-specific** ([bn] onsets fine in Hebrew) — re-check per target market.
- **Encodable:** represent every candidate in **IPA**, tag each segment with articulatory features → drives both sound-symbolism scoring AND pronounceability filtering. Reject/down-score coined names with onset/coda clusters illegal in the target language.

### 4.3 Psycholinguistics (the memorability/recall science)
- Textbooks: Traxler *Introduction to Psycholinguistics*; Harley. Courses (e.g., UCSD LIGN 170).
- Lexical-access models: Logogen, **Cohort** (Marslen-Wilson 1987), TRACE, connectionist/distributed (McClelland & Seidenberg).
- Key effects: **word-frequency effect**; **neighborhood density** (many similar-sounding words slow recognition / cause confusion); **processing fluency** (ease → liking).
- **Encodable:** a good name should be easy to recognize/recall but sit in a **low-confusability phonological neighborhood** (distinct from competitors) while staying wordlike enough to be learned/liked fast.

### 4.4 Morphology / word-formation (the literal generation engine)
- Textbooks: Haspelmath & Sims *Understanding Morphology*; Bauer/Lieber/Plag *Oxford Reference Guide to English Morphology* (e.g., CMU "Words and Word Formation").
- Morpheme (free vs. bound), derivation vs. inflection, and productive coining processes: **compounding, blending/portmanteau** (brunch), **clipping, acronyms/initialisms, back-formation, conversion, derivation (prefix/suffix), neoclassical combining forms** (Latin/Greek roots: -ify, -ex, -io, -ous, -ix, e-).
- **Encodable:** generate by explicitly applying EACH named process and **tag each candidate with the technique used**; use productive affixes/combining forms to make coinages feel like real, derivable words. Compound/blend boundary is acknowledged blurry.

### 4.5 The 5-stage integrated pipeline a "degree-holder" runs
**(1) GENERATE via morphology → (2) FILTER via phonotactics/pronounceability → (3) SCORE via sound symbolism vs. positioning → (4) VALIDATE via psycholinguistics (recall, neighborhood, fluency) → (5) CLASSIFY/SAFETY-CHECK via onomastics/etymology + cross-language.**

## 5. Semiotics, Branding Theory & Consumer Psychology (the "why it works" layer)

- **Saussure:** sign = signifier (form) + signified (concept); the link is **arbitrary/conventional** — meaning is *built by association*, which is the lever naming manipulates.
- **Peirce trichotomy:** symbol (arbitrary — most invented names) / icon (resembles — onomatopoeia, sound-symbolic names) / index (causally connected). **Push names toward iconicity** (sound symbolism) so an arbitrary name *feels* connected.
- **Barthes — denotation vs. connotation vs. myth:** evaluate names on **connotation** (2nd-order cultural/emotional associations), not just literal meaning; reject names whose connotations conflict with positioning even if the denotation fits.
- **Keller CBBE pyramid:** Salience → Meaning → Response → Resonance. A name is the primary driver of **Salience** (awareness/recall) — its first job is to make the brand recognizable/recallable. Brand-element criteria: **memorability, meaningfulness, likability, transferability, adaptability, protectability** (score every candidate on these six; sound-symbolic fit + fluency feed the memorability/meaningfulness sub-scores).
- **Aaker brand equity:** loyalty, awareness (recognition→recall→top-of-mind→dominance), perceived quality, associations, proprietary assets (trademarks). Names mainly serve **awareness + associations**, and become a proprietary asset only if trademarkable.
- **Jennifer Aaker brand personality (tag each name to one):** Sincerity (Disney, Coca-Cola), Excitement (Red Bull, Nike), Competence (IBM, Volvo), Sophistication (Chanel, Mercedes), Ruggedness (Levi's, Jeep, Harley).
- **Processing fluency:** easy-to-process (pronounceable, easy-to-spell, short) names are judged more true, more familiar, more likable, less risky — an automatic halo *before* meaning is parsed.

## 6. Sound Symbolism / Phonosemantics — the encodable science with effect-size humility

### 6.1 The phoneme → connotation maps (academically grounded)
- **VOWELS (strongest, most replicated layer) — Klink 2000, Sapir mil/mal:** Front/high vowels (/i/ "ee", /e/) → small, light, fast, sharp, mild, feminine, precise. Low/back vowels (/u/, /o/, /a/) → large, heavy, slow, rich, rounded, masculine.
- **CONSONANTS (secondary modifier) — Klink 2000:** Fricatives (f, s, v, z) + voiceless (p, t, k) → small/fast/light/sharp/feminine. Stops/plosives (b, d, g) + voiced → large/slow/heavy/solid.
- **bouba/kiki (shape/personality):** rounded sonorants (b, m, l, n) + rounded vowels (o, u) → soft/organic/friendly; voiceless plosives (k, t, p) + front /i/ → sharp/edgy/fast/techy.
- **Taste/sensory (Spence crossmodal):** sweet/smooth/creamy → sonorant + rounded (m, l, n, o, u); bitter/sour/salty/fizzy → angular voiceless plosives (k, t, p, i).
- **Lexicon's working shorthand (from Placek):** **V** = most alive/vibrant/daring (Corvette, Viagra, Vercel); **B** = most reliable/trustworthy (BlackBerry); **T** = reliability; **Z** = attention/energy (Azure); **X** = innovation; soft **m/h/w/l/r/n** = comforting/calming (Marriott, Hilton, L'Oréal).

### 6.2 The empirical anchors
- **Yorkston & Menon 2004 (JCR):** "Frosh" (back /o/) rated creamier/smoother/richer than "Frish" (front /i/). Effects are **automatic, outside awareness, STRONGER under cognitive load / scarce info** → weight sound symbolism heavily for impulse/low-consideration/first-impression categories; less for high-deliberation B2B/expert buyers with rich diagnostic info.
- **Lowrey & Shrum 2007 (JCR) — THE critical moderator (MATCH RULE):** sound symbolism improves *preference* only when the connoted attribute is the single MOST DESIRABLE attribute for that category. A convertible/knife benefits from front-vowel (small/sharp); an SUV/hammer is HURT by it. **Mismatched symbolism actively lowers preference.** Step 1: identify the category hero attribute; Step 2: pick phonemes that connote it.
- **Alter & Oppenheimer 2006 (PNAS) — fluency pays, then decays:** fluently-named IPO stocks and pronounceable tickers (KAR > RDO) outperformed; advantage was largest at launch ($85.35 excess after 1 day) and **shrank over time** ($20.25 after 1 year) as real info accumulated → weight pronounceability most for new/unknown brands.
- **Keller 1993 (CBBE):** memorable/meaningful names build equity faster (raise awareness, seed favorable associations) before marketing spend.

### 6.3 Effect-size & safety caveats (do NOT treat as laws)
- **bouba/kiki is ~70% cross-culturally, not 95%.** Cwiek et al. 2022 (25 languages): posterior mean 72% (CI 56–82%); bouba matched round in 22/25 languages but kiki in only 11/25; Romanian (36%), Mandarin, Turkish fell below chance — often due to native-word interference (Romanian *buba* = sore). 95% figures are WEIRD lab samples.
- **Phonotactics gate the effect.** Styles & Gawne 2017: meta-analysis 89% congruent overall, but Syuba (Nepal) and Hunjara (PNG) showed NO effect; the effect **collapses when test nonwords aren't phonotactically legal** in the audience's language.
- **Rule:** treat all phoneme→attribute mappings as **probabilistic majority tendencies (~60–75% cross-culturally)**, verify against the target audience's language, screen for native homophones, and always output a **ranked shortlist with rationale**, never a single "correct" name.

## 7. Trademark / IP Law Fundamentals (the hard gate)

### 7.1 Distinctiveness spectrum (Abercrombie v. Hunting World) — the #1 encodable legal axis
**Fanciful (coined: Kodak, Exxon, Polaroid) > Arbitrary (real word, unrelated: Apple, Amazon) > Suggestive (needs imagination: Netflix, Coppertone, Microsoft, Airbus) > Descriptive (states a feature: Best Buy, "Creamy") > Generic (the category word: Milk, Bicycle).**
- Fanciful + arbitrary = strongest, inherently distinctive, best protectable.
- **Suggestive = the practical sweet spot** (inherently distinctive AND conveys marketing meaning).
- **Descriptive** = unregistrable on the Principal Register unless it acquires **secondary meaning**; weak/hard to enforce.
- **Generic** = NEVER protectable; beware **genericide** (Aspirin, Escalator, Thermos).
- **Imagination test** to distinguish suggestive vs. descriptive: a required mental leap = suggestive (good); immediate/direct = descriptive (weak).
- Treat **surnames and geographic terms** as weak/hard-to-protect (like descriptive) and flag them.

### 7.2 Nice Classification
45 classes: **1–34 goods, 35–45 services.** A registration can span multiple classes. Determines which prior marks matter, because confusion turns partly on relatedness of goods/services.

### 7.3 Likelihood of confusion — the two-part rule
Confusion requires **similar marks AND related goods/services** (neither alone suffices). Marks compared on **appearance, sound, connotation, commercial impression.** Decided case-by-case via the **13 DuPont factors** — the first two dominate: (1) similarity of marks; (2) similarity/nature of goods/services. Others: trade channels; buyer conditions (impulse vs. sophisticated); **fame of the prior mark (factor 5 — famous marks get broader protection)**; number of similar marks in use; actual confusion; concurrent use; etc.

### 7.4 Anti-dissection rule
Compare marks as **wholes** (overall commercial impression), but give **more weight to the dominant/salient portion.** Adding a generic/descriptive word ("Tech," "Pro," "Co") to someone else's mark does **NOT** cure confusion. **Doctrine of foreign equivalents:** translate common-language foreign words to English to test genericness/descriptiveness/similarity.

### 7.5 Clearance is two-tier
1. **Knockout/screening search** — quick check for identical/near-identical marks in the target Nice class(es), done EARLY (before deep evaluation and before client presentation).
2. **Comprehensive search** — similar (not just identical) marks across federal (USPTO), state, and **common-law sources** (domains, social handles, business directories, industry pubs), accounting for **spelling variants, phonetic equivalents, translations.**
- Output a per-candidate risk label: **Strong / Caution-descriptive / Reject-generic / Conflict-risk** + the Nice class checked.

### 7.6 Domain doctrine (modern consensus)
Trademark > URL. **".com is just an area code"** — do NOT block name selection on exact-match .com; pick the right name, then acquire the domain. Still check domain + social-handle availability during screening; offer a "name + matching .com + pre-screened TM" productized low-tier SKU.

## 8. Heuristics Beyond the Basics (the contrarian / expert layer)

1. **Name for BEHAVIOR and the FUTURE, never describe.** The brief must capture desired future customer behavior + the response you want. Hard-penalize literal/descriptive/suggestive-toward-literal candidates (Diehard "boxed itself in").
2. **Quantity breeds quality — naming is intentionally inefficient.** 1,000–1,500+ raw (often 2,000–3,000); of ~3,000 maybe ~250 "diamonds," → 50–100 candidates → present 15–20. Strictly separate **diverge (judgment BANNED, "let's not reject anything today") from converge (score/filter).**
3. **Three-team / disguised-brief method.** Team A = real brief; Team B = thinks it's naming a competitor; Team C = told it's an unrelated category (e.g., "name a bike"). The unrelated team yields lateral, ownable names (Windsurf came from a team just listing "flow" concepts, not thinking about IDEs).
4. **Strategy-before-words "diamond."** Label: **Win** (top), **What we have to win** (right), **What we need to win** (bottom), **What we need to SAY** (left). Generate and score against this, not features.
5. **Embrace discomfort — polarization is a sign of STRENGTH.** Comfort/consensus = you don't have the right name yet. Pentium (Andy Grove: "the polarization tells me there's energy"), Azure ("a dumb name"), Sonos ("not entertainment enough"), BlackBerry ("crazy") were all initially rejected. Down-weight bland/comfortable candidates; tell the user a split reaction means energy.
6. **"Surprising, but surprisingly familiar."** The brain is lazy — recombine familiar parts/compounds in unexpected ways (PowerBook, BlackBerry, Windsurf = 1+1=3). Avoid both literal descriptors and unpronounceable gibberish.
7. **Define the name's emotional JOB — often counter-position against the product's downside.** BlackBerry's job was to *calm* an anxiety-inducing email device (evoke summer/fresh fruit), not describe it.
8. **Mine the use-case VERBS and transform real words via clipping/respelling/affixing.** Swiffer: swipe → Swif (dropped T, added F, "more verbal," made it sound "a bit more fun, almost like a toy"); screened out "mop" (negative associations).
9. **Morpheme/affix library that sums to the promise.** Pentium = "pent" (strength, cf. Pentagon) + "-ium" (scientific/elemental, cf. titanium/sodium). Compose coined names whose parts' connotations ADD to the brand promise.
10. **Coined ≠ neutral — infer & TEST the implied connotation.** Dasani had no meaning but "sounded like a spa"; validated by telling spa visitors it was a new spa ("healthy," "relaxing"). Test coined names in a neutral/disguised frame.
11. **Make ONE thing communicate well.** The more jobs you assign a name, the smaller the target (A Hundred Monkeys). Penalize multi-message candidates.
12. **Forgettability is the worst failure.** "The worst crime in marketing: not getting noticed." Make memorability/noteworthiness the #1 objective — "it's simple to influence how people make up their minds, but you must influence what they remember."
13. **Fresh-eyes pause.** After shortlisting, re-review a day or two later before deciding (Catchword).
14. **Research is a data point, not a decision point.** Test in realistic context (mockups), against positioning attributes — NOT by asking which names people *like* (favors literal/familiar names) and NOT via focus groups (use one-on-one interviews). Don't ask "is this memorable?" — expose, then follow up on recall weeks later. Testing *depresses* perception vs. post-launch reality (a launched name has validity conferred). "Great brand names are intentional misfits within their categories."
15. **Immersion ↔ incubation cycles** trigger breakthroughs (Shore). Use small teams, not large brainstorms, for invention.
16. **When NOT to rename.** Only rename to solve a real business problem with a clear customer benefit ("what's in it for me") AND internal consensus. Valid trigger = business-model change or going global. If stakeholders split (3 want to / 3 don't) the project is doomed — advise against; renaming for a "shakeup" is a trap.
17. **Final-selection gate: write the internal launch announcement BEFORE choosing.** "We chose X because it does A, B, C and takes us into the future." If you can't write a compelling announcement, the name is wrong.
18. **AI usage rule.** Use AI/small models for high-volume generation + rubric scoring (objectivity on large candidate sets); keep strategy, the discomfort/polarization judgment, sound-symbolism judgment, and legal/cultural clearance as human-grade differentiators.

## 9. 2026 Reality — Trademark Saturation (the dominant constraint)

- US Class 9 (tech): ~23,000 marks when Lexicon started → **well over 2 million today**, against a dictionary of ~500,000 words, searched across 35+ markets. "We're at an inflection point"; clearance may be "almost impossible" within 3–4 years.
- **Implications:** treat clearance as a **first-class, EARLY hard filter**; **name 3 years ahead**; negotiate licensing; bias hard toward **coined/distinctive** (more clearable) over descriptive; don't block on .com.

## 10. Master Encodable Checklist (the consolidated rubric)

**Intake (do first):** written brief/positioning — what the company stands for (not just does), audience, competitors (run a naming audit), desired name styles/territories, the **diamond** (Win / have-to-win / need-to-win / need-to-SAY), the brand PROMISE, point of difference, and desired customer BEHAVIOR/feeling. Identify the category's single HERO attribute (for the sound-symbolism match rule). Write explicit "name objectives."

**Generate (judgment banned):** 500–2,000+ candidates across name-type territories and morphological processes (compound/blend/clip/affix/neoclassical), from multiple frames (literal + competitor + unrelated). Mile-wide-inch-deep, then inch-wide-mile-deep.

**Score finalists on:**
1. Memorability / noteworthiness (top priority) + low-confusability neighborhood
2. Processing ease / pronounceability / spellability (IPA, legal phonotactics) — weight highest for new brands
3. Category distinctiveness (white-space vs. competitors)
4. Fit-to-promise / behavior / target personality (Aaker dimension) / connotation (Barthes)
5. Sound-symbolism MATCH to the hero attribute (not just any symbolism)
6. Keller's six brand-element criteria (memorable, meaningful, likable, transferable, adaptable, protectable)
7. SMILE positives / SCRATCH disqualifiers

**Gate (hard filters):** distinctiveness spectrum (reject generic, flag descriptive/surname/geographic), Nice-class-aware trademark knockout BEFORE presentation (sight/sound/meaning, phonetic + foreign-equivalent variants), cross-language/cultural + homophone screen, domain/social (not a veto).

**Deliver:** shortlist 5–25 + per-name rationale tied to the brief + in-context mockups + per-candidate risk label. Final gate: auto-draft the internal launch announcement; if no compelling story, reject.
